01 · The Learning Experience

Module 1 became a world, not a worksheet

Twelve missions, 154 screens, sixteen hands-on activities. Kids don't read about AI — they poke it, train it, and draw for it.

"AI or Not?" Arcade — Mission 1's new finale

Twelve everyday things zoom in one at a time — smart speaker, toaster, YouTube recommendations — and the kid calls it: AI, or just a machine? Seven-second timer, score, streak multipliers, and an instant one-line "why" after every call. Module 1 now opens with a bang.

Draw for a real AI live AI

Kids draw on a canvas and an actual vision model looks at their pixels and guesses the picture. Not a simulation — the exact concept Mission 5 teaches, demonstrated on the kid's own art.

Three more concept games

Word Chain (build a sentence by picking next words with visible probabilities — how language models actually work), Training Pipeline (put the steps of training in order), and Light the Network (spend limited strength points on connections to make a signal reach the output — weights, made tactile).

Video breaks

Six short Code.org "How AI Works" videos embedded at exactly the right moments, in privacy mode.

Mission art that explains itself AI-generated

The abstract diagrams are gone. Every mission opens with a picture-book scene of a kid and robot doing that mission's concept, plus a caption that lands the idea ("Training = showing a computer LOTS of examples…").

Real AI history, license-verified

Mission 9 is now a photo gallery: Ada Lovelace, Alan Turing, the actual 1966 ELIZA chat transcript, Shakey the robot, and a Deep Blue tower — all public-domain or Creative Commons, credits included.

Audio learning

One toggle and every screen reads itself aloud in a warm narrator voice (ElevenLabs) — for early readers and ear-first learners. Music ducks underneath automatically.

Study Beats — an endless lo-fi radio generated live

A music dock composes lo-fi beats in real time — no audio files, no licensing. Kids pick vibes, change tempo, add vinyl crackle or rain, and remix the whole beat. As of last night it evolves while it plays: new drum patterns every section, improvised melodies, gentle key changes. It is itself a generative-AI lesson.

02 · Progression & Pacing

Designed so kids come back

Drip pacing in three parts

Missions 1–4 open on day one; 5–8 unlock two days later; 9–12 two days after that — anchored to each cohort's start date. Finishing a part earns a warm "your brain needs time to let this settle" screen, not a wall. Parents always have a reason to send kids back.

Badges + character gear

Every mission awards custom AI-generated badge art plus a new accessory for the kid's avatar — capes, jetpacks, crowns, a champion's laurel. Avatars are alive now: they blink, bob, and get excited on hover.

Mission Map: replay and fast-forward

A map button in the player lists every mission — finished ones replay, the current one jumps to your exact spot, locked parts show their unlock day. Progress is bulletproof now: a "furthest-reached" marker syncs to the cloud, so switching devices or clearing a browser can never lose a kid's progress.

03 · Community & Safety

A clubhouse with guardrails

Live chat + private messages

Cohort-wide chat plus one-tap private messages: tap any member's avatar, see their profile card, hit "Chat with…". An "online now" strip shows who's around, with live green dots on the roster.

Profanity filtering at two layers

Blocked in the browser before sending and enforced again inside the database — it cannot be bypassed by clever kids. Chat notes remind everyone that staff can see all messages, including private ones.

FAQ & AI Dictionary

Twenty kid-voiced Q&As on the board: how the platform works, plus AI terms explained for ages 8–11.

One consistent platform shell

Every page shares a branded header — logo, Home / Missions / Board / Profile pills, breadcrumbs — so kids always know where they are and how to get home.

05 · Mission Control — for you and Jon

The staff console

Cohort progress at a glance

Every kid: avatar, current mission, progress bar, badge art, last-active time — plus signals that flag who needs attention (not started / quiet 3+ days / behind the unlocked part). Each row has a DM button straight into a private chat.

An inbox that knows what's unanswered

Private messages to staff are flagged "reply" until you respond; Help & Questions posts are flagged until a staff member comments.

Slack notifications — live

The moment a kid sends staff a private message or posts in Help, a Slack ping lands with the kid's name, a preview, and a reply link. Confirmed working this morning.

Access
