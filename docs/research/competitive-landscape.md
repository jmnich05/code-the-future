## Executive summary

1. **"Learn to code for kids" is crowded and largely commoditized.** Free incumbents
   (Code.org, Scratch) plus a thick layer of paid platforms (Tynker, CodeSpark, Kodable,
   Juni) and physical franchises (Code Ninjas, iD Tech) blanket the market. Competing here
   on "we teach coding" is a losing game.
2. **Dedicated AI *literacy* for 8–11-year-olds is thin** — and what exists is mostly
   *free classroom curriculum* (MIT's Day of AI) or *creative prompt-craft* (CodeWizardsHQ),
   not a consumer product with a community. Most "AI for kids" skews **older** (middle/high
   school) or starts at ~grade 4.
3. **Almost no one frames learning around AI *agents* and "how the nature of work is
   changing" for this age band.** The one player doing genuine agent-building (iD Tech)
   targets **teens (13–17)**. For 8–11, the agentic / future-of-work framing is **open white
   space.**
4. **The incumbents are validating the thesis — but aiming older.** Code.org is publicly
   pivoting from "learn to code for a career" toward "understand how the technology works,"
   teaching kids to **read and debug AI-generated code after learning foundations** `[3]` —
   strikingly close to Code the Future's *"code runs the loop, AI handles the judgment,
   humans handle exceptions."* They're just pointing it at high school (AI Foundations,
   due ~fall 2026 `[3]`). **Code the Future's wedge is owning that same idea for 8–11 first.**
5. **A blended "platform-at-home + local in-person cohort" model is differentiated.** Rivals
   are either online-only (Create & Learn, CodeWizardsHQ, Tynker), franchise-center-based
   (Code Ninjas), or pure curriculum (Day of AI). A home platform + a **local library
   cohort with mentors** (psychologist-designed, small founding group) is a community moat
   national platforms can't easily copy.

**One-line positioning:** *Code the Future is the only program teaching 8–11-year-olds to be
AI-native operators — understanding agents and the changing nature of work — through a
home platform plus a local, mentored cohort.*

## 1 · Online kids' coding platforms (and how they're adding AI)

The base layer of the market — large, mature, and now scrambling to add AI.

- **Code.org** — the dominant free nonprofit, K–12, used widely in schools. It is **pivoting
  hard to AI**: it has leaned its annual "Hour of Code" toward an **"Hour of AI,"** shipped
  an **"Exploring Generative AI"** unit with Amazon (Feb 2025) for **grades 8–12** `[2]`, is
  building an **"AI Foundations"** course for **high school (grades 9–12), due ~fall 2026**,
  with middle-school AI planned `[3][8]`. Its 2024–25 updates added AI *across* existing
  K–12 coding curricula plus standalone modules, including a younger **"How AI Makes
  Decisions"** module (≈grade 3+, ~4 hrs) `[1]`. Pedagogy is shifting to **foundations
  first, then read/debug AI-generated code** `[3]`. **Takeaway:** the biggest free player is
  validating Code the Future's exact thesis — but its real AI depth is **middle/high
  school**; 8–11 AI is light.
- **Scratch (MIT)** — free, beloved, foundational block-coding (~8–16). No real AI literacy;
  it's the creative-coding substrate many others build on.
- **Tynker** — paid self-serve platform (~5–17), game/Minecraft-flavored, has **bolted on AI
  features** `[4]`. Breadth over depth; no agent/work framing.
- **CodeSpark Academy / Kodable** — early-elementary (pre-readers → ~10), puzzle-based
  coding. Minimal AI. Good at the *youngest* end Code the Future touches.
- **Khan Academy** — free; notable mainly for **Khanmigo**, an AI *tutor* — i.e., AI as a
  teaching tool, **not** teaching kids *about* AI.

**Read:** crowded, mostly self-serve, free-to-cheap, and AI is arriving as **features bolted
onto coding** rather than an integrated AI-native pedagogy.

## 2 · Online kids' AI-literacy programs (the thinner shelf)

- **MIT Day of AI / RAISE** `[7][12]` — the most credible **dedicated kids' AI-literacy**
  effort: **free, ready-to-teach, K–12**, with grade-banded units including **Lower
  Elementary (8–10)** and Middle (11–13). It's **concept-first** ("What is AI?", "How Do
  Machines Learn?", bias, ethics) — *not* coding-with-AI-bolted-on. **But:** it's a **school
  curriculum, not a consumer product/community**, future-of-work/careers content **skews
  16–18**, and agentic content is limited (chatbots / "The Brain Behind the Bot") `[7]`.
  → The closest philosophical analog to Code the Future's *content*, aimed at a similar age —
  but a totally different delivery model and missing the agent/work frame.
- **Create & Learn** `[5][13]` — live online **small-group (3–5)** classes, with a real
  multi-level **AI pathway** ("AI Explorers," "Create with ChatGPT"). **But** the AI track
  **starts at ~grade 4 (age 9–10)** — no dedicated track for the youngest 8-year-olds — and
  topics are computer vision / generative AI / ML-with-Python, **not agents or future-of-work** `[13]`.
- **CodeWizardsHQ** `[10]` — live online; notably has **"Art of AI Prompting" for ages 8–10**
  — prompt engineering for images/music. Real, age-appropriate, but framed as **creative
  prompt-craft**, not agents or the changing nature of work.
- **Others worth tracking** (named in the brief; lighter footprint for *young* kids):
  **AI4ALL** (high-school/diversity pipeline), **Inspirit AI** (high-school), **ReadyAI**
  (K–12 nonprofit, competitions), **Cognimates / Machine Learning for Kids / Google AI
  Experiments** (free tinkering tools, hobbyist), **Common Sense Education** (AI-literacy &
  digital-citizenship lessons — useful classroom material, not a product). Most skew older
  or are tools rather than programs.

**Read:** genuine AI *literacy* for 8–11 is **rare and mostly free/school-based or creative**.
No consumer brand owns "AI-native for young kids" with a community.

## 3 · In-person delivery & how it's managed

How national players actually staff and run kids' tech training:

- **Code Ninjas** `[15]` — **franchise** model, physical "dojos" across US/Canada/UK.
  Coding-through-games (Roblox, Minecraft, robotics, YouTube), belt-style progression,
  membership + camps. **Added an "AI Academy" as a bolt-on.** Ops = franchisee-owned centers,
  local part-time "Senseis," corporate curriculum. **Scales via franchising; AI is an
  add-on, not the core.**
- **iD Tech** `[9]` — large national camp operator (in-person at university campuses +
  online). Strong, current AI offerings incl. **genuine AI-agent building** (inject a chatbot
  into your code; build custom agents) — **but ages 13–17**. Ops = seasonal hired
  instructors, campus venues, premium weekly pricing (~$900–1,600/wk in-person).
- **theCoderSchool / Coding with Kids / iCode / Snapology / Galileo** — a mix of
  franchise + corporate after-school/camp providers; instructor-led, small groups,
  curriculum-from-HQ. Coding-first; AI is nascent/bolt-on.
- **Museums / science centers & public libraries** — host coding clubs, Scratch/robotics
  workshops, and increasingly AI events; **volunteer or staff-led, free or low-cost,
  community-anchored.** This is the lane Code the Future's **library cohort** plays in — but
  with a *structured curriculum + home platform + mentor model* most library programs lack.

**Read:** two dominant in-person models — **franchise centers** (Code Ninjas) and **seasonal
camps** (iD Tech) — plus a fragmented community layer (libraries/museums). None pair a
**persistent home platform** with a **local, mentored, cohort-based** community for this age.

## 4 · The core question: who combines coding + AI + agents / future-of-work?

| Dimension | Who's there | Gap for 8–11 |
|---|---|---|
| Teaches coding to 8–11 | Everyone | None — crowded |
| Teaches **AI literacy** to 8–11 | Day of AI, Create & Learn (gr4+), CodeWizardsHQ | Thin; mostly free/creative |
| **Integrates** coding **+** AI as one pedagogy (not bolt-on) | Code.org (foundations→read/debug AI code) | **Aimed at MS/HS, not 8–11** |
| Frames around **AI agents** | iD Tech (build agents) | **Ages 13–17 only** |
| Frames around **"how the nature of work is changing"** | Code.org's *mission* shift `[3]`; scattered | **No one for 8–11** |
| **Blended** home-platform **+ local in-person cohort** | — | **Essentially no one** |

**The finding:** plenty combine coding + a little AI; a few teach real AI literacy young;
**one** does agent-building (teens); and **nobody** brings *agents + the changing nature of
work* to **8–11-year-olds**, let alone through a **blended local-cohort model.** That
intersection is Code the Future's white space.

## 5 · White space & closest competitors

- **Closest on *content philosophy*:** **Code.org** (foundations-first, read/debug AI code,
  mission reframed to "understand the tech as AI changes work") — but free, school-channel,
  and **older**. They prove the thesis is right; they're not serving 8–11 with it.
- **Closest on *young-kids AI literacy*:** **MIT Day of AI** (concept-first, 8–10 band) —
  but a free classroom curriculum, no product/community, no agent/work frame.
- **Closest on *young-kids AI as a product*:** **CodeWizardsHQ "Art of AI Prompting" (8–10)**
  and **Create & Learn's AI pathway (gr4+)** — but creative/tool-focused, online-only, no
  agents/work framing, no local community.
- **Closest on *in-person community*:** **Code Ninjas** (local centers) and **libraries** —
  but coding-games / ad-hoc, AI bolted on at best.

**No single competitor occupies all four corners** (young + AI-native + agent/work-framed +
blended-local). Code the Future does.

## 6 · Positioning recommendations

1. **Don't sell "learn to code." Sell "grow up AI-native."** Coding is the *substrate*; the
   product is *kids who understand AI and the changing world of work.* The free incumbents
   own "learn to code" — cede it and reframe above it.
2. **Plant the flag on agents + future-of-work for young kids — now.** It's genuinely open,
   and it's where the incumbents are heading (older) and will eventually come down-age. The
   *"code does the loop, AI does the judgment, humans handle exceptions"* thesis is both a
   real differentiator **and** independently validated by Code.org's pivot `[3]`.
3. **Make the blended local cohort the moat.** A national platform can copy content; it
   can't copy a Louisville library cohort with named mentors, a child-psychologist
   co-founder's design, and small-group community. Lead marketing with the *local + human*
   story.
4. **Price in the gap between free and premium.** Free (Code.org/Day of AI/Scratch) anchors
   the floor; premium live (Juni ~$250–300/mo `[6]`, iD Tech ~$900–1,600/wk `[9]`, Code
   Ninjas ~$150–300/mo) anchors the ceiling. The **platform + in-person bundle** justifies a
   **mid-premium** the pure-online players can't match on experience.
5. **Move before the incumbents down-age.** Code.org's middle-school AI and the broader
   "AI for kids" wave are coming. The window to own **8–11 AI-native** is **now** (2026).

## 8 · Fast-movers to watch (the space is shifting under us)

- **Code.org's AI push** — Hour of AI, AI Foundations (HS, ~fall 2026), planned MS AI, and a
  publicly reframed mission `[3][8]`. The most important competitor to track; watch for any
  **down-age** move into elementary AI.
- **Incumbents bolting on AI** — Code Ninjas "AI Academy," Tynker AI features, etc. Expect
  more "+AI" announcements; most will be shallow add-ons (an opportunity to out-depth them).
- **Prompt-craft creep** — CodeWizardsHQ-style "AI prompting for kids" will proliferate;
  differentiate by going beyond prompting into *agents, judgment, and how work changes.*
