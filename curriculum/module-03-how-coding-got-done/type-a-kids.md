## How this module works (read this first)

Welcome back, builder! In Module 1 you learned *what* AI is. In Module 2 you saw AI
*out in the world*. This module is about something closer to home: **how the apps, games,

Here's the deal. Every screen you've ever tapped was made of **code** — instructions a human
wrote, one careful line at a time. For a long, long time, that was slow, tricky work. Then,
almost overnight, AI learned to write code *with* people. The job of a builder is changing

Each mission is about 10 minutes: a quick read, a video or picture, and a hands-on game where

## Mission 1 — What Even Is Code?

*~10 minutes · Mission 1 of 12*

Look around at the screens in your life — the game you play, the video app, the smart watch.
Every single one is run by **code**: a list of exact instructions telling the computer what
to do, step by step.

Here's the most important secret about computers: they are incredibly fast, but they are *not*
clever on their own. A computer will do *exactly* what you tell it — no more, no less. If you
tell it the wrong thing, it does the wrong thing, instantly and perfectly. So writing code is
really about thinking *very* clearly and putting steps in *exactly* the right order.

You already think in code more than you'd guess. A recipe is code: "crack the egg, *then* whisk
it, *then* pour it in." Directions to a friend's house are code: "go straight, turn left at the
park, it's the third door." A computer just needs those steps spelled out with zero guessing,
because it won't fill in the blanks the way a person would.

A list of clear steps that gets a job done has a special name: an **algorithm**. That's a fancy
word for something you already do every day. Brushing your teeth? Algorithm. Tying your shoes?
Algorithm. Coding is the art of writing algorithms a computer can follow.

## Mission 2 — Talking to Computers

*~10 minutes · Mission 2 of 12*

Computers don't actually understand English (or any human language). Deep down, they only
understand electricity — billions of tiny on-and-off switches. So how do people tell them what
to do? With a **coding language**: a special, simple language that's halfway between how we talk
and how a computer thinks.

There isn't just *one* coding language — there are hundreds! Just like people speak Spanish,
Arabic, or Japanese, computers can be programmed in **Python**, **HTML**, **C++**, and many more.
Each one has its own words and rules, and each is good at different jobs. Don't worry about
memorizing them — just know that a coding language is how a human writes down an algorithm so a
computer can run it.

Here's a friendly one. In **Python**, to make the computer say hello, you write:

`print("Hello!")`

That's it. `print` means "show this on the screen," and the words in the quotes are what to
show. Simple, right? Coding languages are built to be *learnable* — they use real-ish words on
purpose so humans can read them.

The big idea: a coding language is a **translator**. You write something a human can almost read,
and the language turns it into the on-off switches the computer actually needs. For a long time,
humans had to write every one of those lines by hand — and that's the story of the next mission.

## Mission 3 — The Old Way: By Hand

*~10 minutes · Mission 3 of 12*

Let's travel back in time — to, oh, about three years ago. If you wanted to build an app or a
game, there was only one way: a human typed **every single line of code by hand**. Thousands of
lines. Tens of thousands. One. At. A. Time.

It was slow and careful work. A programmer would picture what they wanted, break it into tiny
steps, and write each step in just the right order with just the right words. Miss a comma, spell
something wrong, or put two lines out of order, and the whole thing wouldn't run. So they'd write
a bit, test it, fix it, write a bit more, test it again — for hours, sometimes for months.

Think about that. The apps on your tablet, the games on your console — real people sat and typed
them out, line by line, fixing tiny mistakes along the way. It took years of practice to get
good, and even the experts spent a *lot* of their day just hunting for what they typed wrong.

This wasn't bad — it was amazing! Everything digital you've ever used was built this way. But it
was *hard*, and it was *slow*, and only people who had spent years learning a coding language
could do it at all. Hold onto that feeling — because in just a couple of missions, you'll see how
wildly that changed.

## Mission 4 — Bugs!

*~10 minutes · Mission 4 of 12*

Here's a funny-but-true word every coder knows: a mistake in code is called a **bug**. When your
program does the wrong thing — or won't run at all — you've got a bug, and finding it is called
**debugging**.

Why "bug"? Long ago, a real moth got stuck inside a giant computer and stopped it from working.
A scientist taped the moth into her notebook and wrote "first actual bug found." The name stuck!
Ever since, every glitch, freeze, and weird mistake is a "bug" — and squashing them is a huge part
of a coder's life.

Most bugs are tiny. A missing comma. A misspelled word. Saying "9" when you meant "g." The
computer can't guess what you *meant* — remember, it does *exactly* what you wrote — so one
little slip can stop everything. Coders can spend *hours* hunting for a single wrong character,
like finding one wrong word in a giant book.

But here's the secret that makes you a real builder: **bugs are normal, and they are not failure.**
Every single coder, even the best in the world, writes bugs constantly. The skill isn't *never*
making mistakes — it's staying calm, hunting them down, and fixing them. Mistakes are how you
learn what the computer needs. So when something breaks today, smile: you just found a clue.

`[IMAGE: mission-4-b | A "bug" is a mistake in code — even one missing comma can stop everything. Finding it is called debugging.]`

`[INTERACTIVE: "Bug Hunt" — each program has one tiny bug hiding in it. Find it and fix it to make the code run. They get sneakier each round!]`

### ✅ Mission 4 Complete!
**You just unlocked:**
- A mistake in code is a **bug**; finding and fixing it is **debugging**.
- Every coder makes bugs — the skill is hunting them down, not avoiding them.

You learned that mistakes are part of the job. Real builders embrace them. 🐛

## Mission 5 — The Right Tool for the Job

*~10 minutes · Mission 5 of 12*

Remember how there are *hundreds* of coding languages? Here's why: different jobs need different
tools. You wouldn't use a hammer to paint a wall, and coders don't use one language for
everything. Let's meet a few of the most famous ones — you don't have to memorize them, just get
a feel for who does what.

**Python** 🐍 is friendly and easy to read, and it's the favorite for **AI and working with data**.
When people build smart programs that learn — like the AI you met in Module 1 — they very often
reach for Python.

**HTML** 🌐 is the language of **web pages**. Every website you visit — its words, pictures, and
buttons — is built with HTML. It's less like giving commands and more like building with labeled
blocks: "here's a heading, here's a picture, here's a link."

**C++** ⚡ is fast and powerful, used when speed *really* matters — like **video games** and the
software inside robots and machines. It's trickier to learn, but it's a racecar when you need one.

There are tons more (and you'll meet **SQL** for asking questions of giant piles of data). The
point isn't the names — it's the idea: a great builder picks the **right tool for the job**.
And soon, you'll see, you can even ask an AI which tool fits and have it write in that language
for you.

## Mission 6 — The Big Change

*~10 minutes · Mission 6 of 12*

Okay — here's the part that changed everything. For *decades*, coding meant a human typing every
line by hand. Then, in just the **last couple of years**, something wild happened: **AI learned to
write code.**

Remember from Module 1 how AI learns from tons of examples? Well, people fed AI an enormous amount
of code — millions and millions of programs that humans had already written. By spotting the
patterns, the AI learned how code works. Now you can describe what you want in plain words, and the
AI writes the actual code for you — in seconds.

Think about how big that is. Something that used to take a trained expert *hours* can now happen
in the time it takes to read this sentence. A job that once *only* people who studied for years
could do is suddenly something a curious kid can start doing today — by working *with* an AI.

This is one of the fastest changes in the whole history of technology. It didn't creep in slowly
over fifty years; it arrived in about two. The grown-ups who build software for a living are
learning a brand-new way to work *right now*. And the cool part? You get to learn the new way from
the very beginning — no "old habits" to unlearn.

## Mission 7 — Telling the AI What You Want

*~10 minutes · Mission 7 of 12*

So AI can write code now — but it doesn't read your mind. The way you get great code out of an AI
is by giving it a clear, exact **prompt**: a description of what you want it to build. And here's
the twist that should sound *very* familiar: just like a computer, an AI does best when you tell it
*exactly* what you mean.

Watch what happens with a fuzzy prompt. Say you ask an AI: "make a button." It might make a button…
but what color? How big? What does it do when you click it? The AI has to guess, and it might guess
something you didn't want. Fuzzy prompt, fuzzy result.

Now give it a *clear* prompt: "Make a big green button that says PLAY, and when you click it, the
game starts." Now the AI knows exactly what to build — and it nails it. The clearer your words, the
better the code. Describing what you want, clearly, is a real skill — maybe *the* skill of the new
way of building.

Notice the pattern from this whole journey: a computer does exactly what you type, and an AI does
its best with exactly what you describe. So the most important thing you bring isn't fast typing —
it's **clear thinking** about what you actually want. The AI handles the "how." You handle the
"what."

`[IMAGE: mission-7-b | A clear prompt gets you exactly what you want; a fuzzy prompt makes the AI guess. Clear thinking is the new superpower.]`

## Mission 8 — Agents That Do Things

*~10 minutes · Mission 8 of 12*

A chatbot *answers*. But the newest AIs can *act* — they don't just tell you the answer, they go
and **do the steps**. We call one of these an **agent**, and it's the heart of how building works
now.

Here's the difference. Ask a chatbot "how do I make a website?" and it explains it to you. Ask an
**agent** to "make me a website," and it actually gets to work: it writes the code, tries running
it, notices a bug, fixes the bug, tries again, and keeps going until it's done. It runs a little
loop — **plan, do, check, fix, repeat** — all by itself.

That loop should ring a bell. Back in Module 2 you learned the big rule: *code handles the loop, AI
handles the judgment, humans handle the exceptions.* An agent is AI running a loop of small jobs,
using tools (like writing a file or testing the code), and making little judgment calls along the
way — checking its own work and fixing what's broken.

But here's what matters most: an agent is powerful, but it's **not the boss — you are.** It can run
a hundred steps in a minute, but *you* decide what it should build, and *you* check whether the
result is actually good. The agent is an amazing helper with super-speed hands. The brain, the
goal, and the final "yes, that's right" — those stay with you.

`[IMAGE: mission-8-b | An agent doesn't just answer — it runs a loop: plan, do, check, fix, repeat — until the job is done.]`

`[INTERACTIVE: "Run the Agent" — give an agent a goal and watch its loop. It hits problems and tries to fix them — but it needs YOU to make the calls it can't. Get it to finish the build!]`

### ✅ Mission 8 Complete!
**You just unlocked:**
- An **agent** doesn't just answer — it *does* the steps, running a loop: **plan, do, check, fix, repeat**.
- Agents are fast helpers, but **you're the boss** — you set the goal and check the work.

You met AI agents — the engines of modern building. Next: a whole team of them. 🤖

## Mission 9 — A Day in the Life

*~10 minutes · Mission 9 of 12*

So what does building software actually look like *today*? Picture a modern developer at their
desk. They're not typing code line-by-line for hours anymore. Instead, they're more like the
**director of a team** — a team made of several AI agents, all working at once.

Here's a real-feeling day. The developer gives one agent the job of building a login screen.
While that's running, they hand a second agent a list of bugs to hunt down. A third agent is busy
writing tests to make sure nothing breaks. The developer moves between them — checking work,
answering questions, saying "yes, ship that" or "no, try it this way." Several things happen at
once, and *they* keep it all pointed in the right direction.

It's a bit like being the head chef in a busy kitchen. The chef isn't personally chopping every
vegetable; they're tasting, deciding, directing, and making sure every plate that goes out is
*good*. The cooks (the agents) are fast and skilled, but the chef holds the vision and the
standards.

This is genuinely new. The most valuable builders today aren't the ones who can type the fastest —
the AI types plenty fast. They're the ones who know *what's worth building*, can *tell good work
from sloppy work*, and can *steer a team of agents* to a great result. That's a job you can start
practicing right now.

`[IMAGE: mission-9-b | A modern builder is like a director or head chef — steering several AI agents at once, keeping everything pointed at a great result.]`

## Mission 10 — Taste, Decisions, and Output

*~10 minutes · Mission 10 of 12*

If AI can write the code, hunt the bugs, and run the tests… what's left for the human? Actually,
the *most* important things. They have names worth remembering: **taste, decisions, and output.**

**Taste** is knowing what's *good*. An agent can make ten different versions of a game screen — but
which one is fun? Which one feels right? Which one would *you* actually want to play? AI can make
lots of stuff; a human with taste knows which stuff is worth keeping. That's you.

**Decisions** are the calls only a person should make. *What* should we build, and *why*? Is this
fair? Is it safe? Is it honest? Remember from Module 2 — humans handle the judgment and the
exceptions. An agent can suggest, but a person decides, especially when it matters.

**Output** means *you* are responsible for what finally goes out the door. If an agent writes
something, you don't just trust it — you check it, test it, and put your name on it. The AI helped,
but the work is *yours*, and so is making sure it's actually good and right.

Here's the heart of this whole module: AI changed *how* code gets written, but it didn't change who
it's *for* or who's *in charge*. Machines bring the speed. **You** bring the taste, the decisions,
and the responsibility. That's not a smaller job than coding used to be — it's a bigger, more
human one.

## Mission 11 — Build Something Real

*~10 minutes · Mission 11 of 12*

Enough watching — time to *do it*. In this mission, you'll be the builder and direct a real AI to
make something for you. You bring the idea and the taste; the AI brings the speed. This is the new
way of building, and you're about to try it with your own two hands.

The recipe is everything you've learned: **think clearly** about what you want, write a **clear
prompt**, look at what the AI makes, and then **decide** — is it good? Is it what you pictured? If
not, you don't start over and you don't give up. You say what to change, and you go around the loop
again. Builders call this *iterating* — making something a little better each time around.

Don't aim for perfect on the first try — nobody does, not even the pros. Aim for *better than last
time*. Your first prompt gets you a start; your second makes it sharper; your third makes it shine.
Every loop, *you're* the one steering it toward great. That feeling — describe, look, decide,
improve — is exactly what building feels like now.

Whatever you make today, remember: the AI was your helper, but the idea was yours, the choices were
yours, and the finished thing is *yours*. You didn't just use a tool. You **directed** one. That's
the whole skill — and you've got it.
