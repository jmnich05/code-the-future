# Module 2 — AI and Our World · Curriculum Sketch (kids, ages 8–11)

The mission-by-mission blueprint. Each mission is ~10 minutes and follows the Module 1
rhythm: **lead with the idea → make it concrete with an everyday analogy → a code.org video
where it fits → a hands-on interactive → a Mission Complete badge.** This is a sketch for
Jon (and Kenya) to tweak before Pass 1 writes the full read-aloud copy.

**Legend:** 🎥 video slot · 🎮 interactive · 🎖️ badge · 🟡 `[KENYA REVIEW]` framing flag.

**Voice reminders (same as Module 1):** short sentences, wonder over warning, "you're the
first generation," concrete before abstract, every careful idea ends on what kids *can do*.

---

## Mission 0 — How this module works *(intro)*
Quick re-orientation: *"Welcome back. Last week you learned what AI IS. This week, 12 new
Missions about what AI DOES out in the world — the amazing stuff and the tricky stuff — and
the choices that come with the most powerful tool people have ever built. Collect all 12
badges, then a new Big Mission."* No widget; sets the map.

## Mission 1 — Welcome Back, Future Builder
**Big idea:** you already know the #1 rule (machines follow rules, AI learns from examples);
now we ask the bigger question — *what does a learning machine mean for the whole world?*
**How it's taught:** callback to Module 1 ("remember the toaster vs. the cook?"), then the
turn: a tool this powerful changes more than apps — it changes medicine, work, the planet,
and it hands people some big choices. Tease the wonder *and* the responsibility.
🎥 Short "look how far AI has come" montage *(code.org / AI-in-the-world topic)*.
🎮 **Poll** (mirrors M1's opening poll so we can compare at M12): *"What's the most amazing
thing you think AI could do for the world?"* — options + free text, saved for the recap.
🎖️ **Returning Builder.**

## Mission 2 — AI Is Already Out There
**Big idea:** AI isn't coming someday — it's already woven through the world around you.
**How it's taught:** zoom out from personal AI (video picks, filters) to society-scale AI
(hospitals, farms, weather, maps for whole cities, translating between languages). The
point: it's *everywhere*, quietly, and this week we look at the big places.
**Analogy:** like electricity a hundred years ago — first a novelty, then suddenly behind
everything.
🎥 *(code.org topic: AI in everyday life / society.)*
🎮 **Sort / map** — "Where's the AI?": drag real-world scenes (a hospital scan, a farm
drone, a video app, a paper map) into *"AI is helping here"* vs *"just a plain machine."*
🎖️ **World Spotter.**

## Mission 3 — AI the Doctor's Helper
**Big idea:** AI helps doctors spot sickness early and helps scientists invent new medicine
faster — one of the most hopeful things it does.
**How it's taught:** callback to Mission 1's "teaching a computer to SEE" — the same
pattern-spotting that found your drawing can help a doctor spot something on a scan a tired
human eye might miss. AI doesn't *replace* the doctor; it's the doctor's super-helper. Then:
AI can test millions of medicine ideas to help cure diseases that have hurt people for a
very long time.
**Analogy:** a metal detector for sickness — it beeps so the doctor knows where to look.
🎥 *(code.org topic: AI for good / health.)*
🎮 **Spot-the-pattern mini-game** (vision callback): scan simple "cell" cards, find the few
that don't match — *then the reveal*: this is the kind of pattern-finding that helps real
doctors. Ends on "and a human always makes the final call."
🟡 Keep "diseases" gentle and hopeful — focus on helping and curing, not scary illness.
🎖️ **Helper.**

## Mission 4 — AI and the Planet
**Big idea:** AI helps with giant problems too big for one person — clean energy, weather,
and science.
**How it's taught:** AI helps run power grids so we waste less energy, helps design better
batteries and solar panels, helps predict storms earlier so people stay safe, and helps
scientists understand things like proteins and space. Big problems, big helper.
**Analogy:** a weather forecaster that has watched a billion storms — it gets really good at
guessing what's next.
🎥 *(code.org topic: AI + science / environment.)*
🎮 **Decision mini-game** *(new widget)* — "Power-Up the City": choose how AI shifts energy
across a tiny city (run the wind farm now, store solar for night). See the trade-off meter.
Light, no wrong answers — the point is *AI helps people make big choices*.
🎖️ **Earth Builder.**

## Mission 5 — AI at Work
**Big idea:** AI is changing how grown-ups work — and the camp's core rule explains how:
**code does the loop, AI does the judgment inside the loop, humans handle the exceptions.**
**How it's taught:** AI is great at the repetitive part of a job (reading a thousand
emails, checking a thousand orders) so people can spend time on the parts that need a human
— caring, deciding, creating. Some jobs change, new jobs appear (someone has to *teach* and
*check* the AI — that could be you). Honest but optimistic: jobs change, they don't just
vanish, and humans stay in charge of the hard calls.
**Analogy:** a dishwasher didn't end cooking — it freed the cook to cook more.
🎥 *(code.org topic: AI + jobs / future of work.)*
🎮 **Sort** — "Who does what?": tasks dropped into *AI handles the loop* / *Human handles
the judgment* (e.g., "sort 10,000 photos" → AI; "decide which photo is the most beautiful"
→ human; "flag the weird one for a person" → both). Teaches the mental model by doing it.
🟡 Reassure, don't alarm, on the jobs question — frame as change + new opportunities.
🎖️ **Team Captain.**

## Mission 6 — Every Tool Can Help or Hurt
**Big idea:** a tool isn't good or bad by itself — the *choices people make* with it are.
**How it's taught:** a hammer builds a treehouse or smashes a window. Fire cooks dinner or
burns things. AI is the most powerful tool ever, so the choices matter the most. This is the
hinge of the whole module: from here on we ask not just *can* AI do it, but *should we*, and
*who chooses?*
**Analogy:** the hammer (returns all module as the "tool test").
🎥 *(code.org topic: AI ethics intro / responsible AI.)*
🎮 **Scenario cards** *(new decision widget)* — same AI, two outcomes: "An AI that knows
what you like → recommends a great book / OR → only ever shows you one kind of thing." Kid
picks "helps" or "hurts," gets the *why*. Builds the help/hurt reflex used for the rest.
🟡 Foundational framing mission — review the tone: curious and empowering, never preachy.
🎖️ **Wise Builder.**

## Mission 7 — Your Stuff Is Yours *(privacy)*
**Big idea:** your information is like your belongings — you should get a say in who sees it.
**How it's taught:** AI gets smart by learning from data, and *you* make data all day (what
you watch, where you go, what you type). That can be helpful (it remembers your favorites)
*and* it's something to be thoughtful about. Intro "digital footprint" — the trail you leave.
The empowering turn: you have choices — what to share, what to keep, asking a grown-up.
**Analogy:** your diary vs. the class bulletin board — some things you share, some you keep.
🎥 *(code.org topic: data & privacy for kids.)*
🎮 **"Share or Keep?" decision game** — items slide by (your favorite color, your home
address, your high score, your password); kid sorts into *okay to share* / *keep private* /
*ask a grown-up*. Reveal explains each.
🟡 Privacy without paranoia — practical and calm, with the "ask a trusted grown-up" lifeline.
🎖️ **Privacy Guard.**

## Mission 8 — Who Holds the Biggest Tools? *(concentration of power)* 🟡
**Big idea:** the most powerful AI is expensive and hard to build, so only a few groups have
the biggest tools right now — and that raises a fairness question.
**How it's taught:** the recess analogy — *what if one kid owned every ball, every jump
rope, and the whole field?* Even a nice kid having all of it means everyone else has to ask
them. Powerful AI is a bit like that today: a few big companies and countries have the
strongest tools. Why it matters: tools shape the world, so it matters that lots of people —
not just a few — get to use them and have a say.
🎥 *(code.org topic: AI access / fairness — verify a kid-appropriate fit.)*
🎮 **Balance / fairness scenario** — distribute "AI tools" among a tiny town; watch the
fairness meter when one group hoards vs. when access is shared. No villains — just *fair vs.
lopsided*.
🟡 `[KENYA REVIEW]` — keep it about fairness and sharing, not "evil companies." Concrete,
not cynical.
🎖️ **Fair Play.**

## Mission 9 — Tools, Rules, and Freedom *(power & control — the heaviest mission)* 🟡
**Big idea:** powerful tools can be used to *help* people or to *boss them around* — and
that's the biggest reason it matters who controls AI.
**How it's taught:** Start safe and concrete: good rules help everyone (traffic lights,
taking turns) — rules can be kind. Then the careful turn: a super-powerful tool can also be
used to *watch* people all the time or to *control* what they're allowed to do or say. In
some places in the world, powerful tools get used to boss people around instead of help
them. That's not to scare you — it's *why* people work hard to make sure AI is used to help,
and why your generation getting a say is so important. End firmly on agency and hope:
people, rules, and choices keep tools on the helping side.
**Analogy:** a flashlight helps you find your way home — or gets shined in your face to
boss you around. Same tool, very different choice.
🎥 Optional / *(code.org topic: responsible & safe AI — pick the gentlest available, or
skip the video here and keep it discussion-led.)*
🎮 **"Helps people / Controls people" sort** — scenarios (an AI that helps a city save
energy; an AI that watches everyone all the time) sorted by whether the tool is *helping*
or *bossing*. Reveal lands on "good rules and many voices keep tools helping."
🟡 `[KENYA REVIEW] — highest priority.` This is the surveillance/authoritarian-misuse
mission. Needs her word-by-word pass: keep it concrete, non-scary, no specific countries
named in the kids' copy, always close on safety + agency. Consider an opt-out note for
sensitive kids.
🎖️ **Freedom Keeper.**

## Mission 10 — Who Decides the Future of AI? 🟡
**Big idea:** nobody should decide alone — not one company, not one ruler. People decide
*together*, with rules and voices — and kids are part of that.
**How it's taught:** resolve the tension from 8–9 on a hopeful note. How do people steer
something powerful? They make rules together, they vote, they speak up, grown-ups whose job
is "keep tools fair and safe" get involved — and new builders (you) bring fresh ideas. The
future of AI isn't already decided; it's being decided now, and your voice counts.
**Analogy:** the rules of a big group game everyone plays — they work best when everyone
helps make them, not when one player makes them all.
🎥 *(code.org topic: shaping AI / your role.)*
🎮 **"Make a Rule for AI"** — kid writes/picks one rule they think AI should follow ("always
tell you it's an AI," "never share your secrets"), adds it to a shared cohort wall. Their
voice, literally on the board.
🟡 `[KENYA REVIEW]` — keep "government / who's in charge" age-appropriate and non-partisan;
frame as *people deciding together*, not politics.
🎖️ **Decider.**

## Mission 11 — You Get to Help Build It
**Big idea:** you're not just going to *use* AI — you get to help *build* and *guide* it, and
a good builder needs three things: curiosity, judgment, and kindness.
**How it's taught:** tie the whole module together. AI is powerful (Missions 2–5), it comes
with real choices (6–10), and the people who'll shape it are kids growing up *with* it — you.
What makes a *good* builder isn't just being smart with the tool; it's asking "should we?"
and "is this fair?" and "who could this help or hurt?" That's the operator mindset the whole
camp is about. Curiosity to build, judgment to build *well*, kindness to build for everyone.
🎥 *(code.org topic: kids/creators shaping technology.)*
🎮 **Builder's Code** — kid assembles their own short pledge from a menu ("I'll stay
curious," "I'll think about fairness," "I'll protect people's privacy," "I'll ask when I'm
not sure") + one in their own words. Goes in their profile and the Time Capsule.
🎖️ **Future Builder II.**

## Mission 12 — You Did It!
**Big idea:** look how big you can now think about AI.
**How it's taught:** celebrate + recap the arc (it helps the world / it comes with choices /
you get to help decide). Call back to Mission 1's poll — *"here's what you said AI could do
for the world; now what would YOU build?"* Bridge straight into the Big Mission.
🎮 Light recap quiz **or** revisit-the-poll reflection (compare Mission 1 vs. now).
🎖️ **Module 2 Complete** — full badge set; unlocks the Big Mission.

## Big Mission (capstone) — *Build an AI Helper for the World*

The Module 2 capstone, same shape as Module 1's "Now use AI yourself" (live OpenAI in the
page), themed to the module: kids don't just *use* AI — they design something good with it,
then check it like a responsible builder.

**Flow:**
1. **Pick a problem you care about** — in your home, your school, your city, or the planet
   (lonely new kids, wasted food, litter, helping grandparents). Pick from a menu or write
   your own.
2. **Design your AI Helper** — name it, say what it does, and (callback to Module 1) what
   *examples* it would learn from. Draw it or describe it.
3. **Use real AI** — write a prompt and watch a real AI respond / help build it (the same
   connector as Module 1's capstone), so they *experience* steering the tool, not just
   reading about it.
4. **Ethics checkpoint** *(the Module 2 twist)* — run your helper through three kid-sized
   questions: **Is it fair to everyone? Does it respect people's privacy? Who could it
   accidentally hurt — and how would you fix that?** Revise.
5. **Share** — a one-line pitch to the cohort wall; mentors/family see it at Demo Day.

**Why it matters:** it fuses the two halves of the module — *making* (the wonder) and
*judging* (the responsibility) — into one project, and it's a near-perfect lead-in to the
in-person **Design-an-AI-Helper** activity (activity bank #18) the session before Demo Day.

🟡 `[KENYA REVIEW]` — the problem menu and the ethics-checkpoint wording: keep problems
hopeful and within a kid's world, keep "who could it hurt" constructive (a puzzle to fix,
not a worry).
