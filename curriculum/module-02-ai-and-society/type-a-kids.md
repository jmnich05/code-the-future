## Mission 2 — AI Is Already Out There
*~10 minutes · Mission 2 of 12*

When you think of AI, you probably picture a chatbot or a phone. But AI is *way* bigger
than your screen. It's already woven into the whole world around you, quietly doing jobs
you never see.

farmer's drone checks which plants need water. A weather system scans the skies and warns a

the world.

That's actually why this module matters. When something becomes *that* important and *that*
common, it stops being just a cool toy and starts being something we all have to think about
carefully — how it's used, and whether it's used well. So this week we're going to visit the
big places AI is showing up: hospitals, the planet, jobs, and the giant questions that come
with all of it.

## Mission 3 — AI the Doctor's Helper
*~10 minutes · Mission 3 of 12*

Here's one of the most hopeful things AI does in the whole world: it helps doctors keep
people healthy.

Remember Mission 5 from last week, when you taught a computer to *see* by finding patterns
in pixels? The exact same trick is now helping doctors. A doctor can look at an X-ray or a
scan, but they're human — they get tired, and some patterns are really, really faint. An AI
that has studied *millions* of scans can quietly point and say, "Hey, look right here." It
catches little patterns a tired human eye might miss. Earlier catches mean people get help
sooner — and sometimes that's a very big deal.

Think of it like a **metal detector for sickness**. The metal detector doesn't dig up the
treasure or decide what to do — it just *beeps* so the person knows exactly where to look.
The AI beeps; the doctor looks; the doctor decides. That last part matters: the AI is the
helper, never the boss. A real human doctor always makes the final call, because caring for
a person takes judgment and kindness, and that's a human superpower.

And it gets even more amazing. To invent a new medicine, scientists used to test ideas one
at a time, by hand, for *years*. AI can help test millions of ideas incredibly fast — like
trying a million keys in a lock in the time it used to take to try a few. That's helping
people work on diseases that have hurt families for a very, very long time. Some of the kids
reading this might grow up to be the scientist — with AI as their helper — who finally cracks
one of them.

So when people say "AI is going to change the world," *this* is a big part of what they
mean. A faster, sharper helper for the people whose whole job is helping us.

## Mission 4 — AI and the Planet
*~10 minutes · Mission 4 of 12*

Some problems are too big for any one person to solve alone — like taking care of our whole
planet. Good news: this is another place AI is becoming an amazing helper.

**Clean energy.** Think about all the power a city uses — lights, trains, schools, homes.
Some of it comes from wind and sun, which are clean but tricky, because the wind doesn't
always blow and the sun goes down at night. AI helps figure out the smartest way to use that
energy: store some for later, send some where it's needed *right now*, and waste as little as
possible. It's like a super-careful helper making sure not a single bit of clean power gets
thrown away.

**Weather and safety.** Remember how AI learns from tons of examples? Now imagine an AI
that has studied a *billion* past storms. It gets really good at guessing what a storm will
do next — which means warning people earlier so they can get somewhere safe. Earlier
warnings save lives.

**Giant science puzzles.** Some mysteries are so huge that scientists were stuck on them for
*decades*. Not long ago, an AI helped figure out the shapes of almost every tiny protein
inside living things — a puzzle people had worked on for ages. Cracking puzzles like that
helps us understand nature, grow better food, and invent new things.

Here's the big idea: AI is great at looking at *enormous* amounts of information and finding
the smartest pattern — way more than a person could ever hold in their head. So for problems
that are really about huge numbers — a whole city's power, a whole planet's weather — it's an
incredible teammate. The humans still decide *what we care about and what to do*. The AI
helps us do it better.

## Mission 5 — AI at Work
*~10 minutes · Mission 5 of 12*

You've probably heard a grown-up wonder, "Is AI going to change jobs?" The honest answer is
*yes* — and once you understand *how*, it's a lot less scary and a lot more interesting.

Here's the secret rule, and it's the heart of this whole camp:

> **Code handles the loop. AI handles the judgment inside the loop. Humans handle the
> exceptions.**

Let's unpack that with a real example. Imagine a job where someone has to read ten thousand
customer emails a week. A computer **loop** can zip through all ten thousand. The **AI** can
read each one and make a smart guess — "this one's a happy thank-you, this one's a problem."
And when a weird, tricky, or sad one shows up that needs real care? That's an **exception** —
and a *human* steps in to handle it with kindness and good judgment.

See what happened? The AI didn't *replace* the person. It took the boring, repeating part —
reading the same kind of thing ten thousand times — so the human could spend their time on
the part that actually needs a human: caring, deciding, creating. A **dishwasher** didn't
end cooking. It freed the cook to cook *more*. AI is like that for lots of jobs.

It's true that some jobs will change, and that can feel uncertain — that's a real feeling,
and it's okay to wonder about it. But here's the part that doesn't make the news: new tools
also *create* brand-new jobs. Someone has to teach the AI, check the AI, and decide what it
should and shouldn't do. (Hmm — who's getting really good at understanding AI right now?
*You.*) When your parents were kids, "app maker" and "video creator" weren't jobs at all.

So the future of work isn't "robots take over." It's **teams** — humans and AI, each doing
what they're best at, with the human as the captain making the calls that matter.

## Mission 6 — Every Tool Can Help or Hurt
*~10 minutes · Mission 6 of 12*

Okay — you've seen the amazing side: AI helping doctors, the planet, and people at work.
Now we turn a corner. Because the most powerful tool in the world comes with the most
important *choices* in the world, and a smart builder thinks about both.

Here's the key idea, and it's small enough to fit in your pocket: **a tool isn't good or
bad by itself. The choices people make with it are what matter.**

Think about a **hammer**. A hammer can build a cozy treehouse… or smash a window. Same
hammer. The difference is the person holding it and what they choose to do. Think about
**fire**: it cooks your dinner and keeps you warm, or it burns things down. The fire isn't
good or evil — it depends on how it's used.

AI is exactly the same, just *way* more powerful — which means the choices matter *way*
more. The same AI that recommends you a perfect new book could, if someone set it up that
way, only ever show you one kind of thing and never let you discover anything new. The same
AI that helps a doctor could be used carelessly somewhere it shouldn't be. Same tool —
totally different outcome — depending on the *choices*.

That's why, from here on, we don't just ask *"What can AI do?"* We ask two more grown-up
questions:

1. **Should we?** Just because we *can* do something doesn't always mean it's a good idea.
2. **Who gets to choose?** Who decides how this powerful tool gets used — and is that fair?

You don't need to be scared of these questions. They're actually the *exciting* part,
because they're where humans stay in charge of the future. For the next few Missions, you
get to be a "help or hurt" detective — and you'll be surprised how good you already are at
it.

## Mission 7 — Your Stuff Is Yours
*~10 minutes · Mission 7 of 12*

Time for your first big builder question, and it's one you can actually use *today*:
**privacy**.

Remember how AI gets smart? It learns from **data** — tons of examples. Here's the thing:
*you* make data all day long, just by living your life. What you watch, what you search,
where you go, what you type, what time you wake up. Little trails of information, everywhere
you go online. Grown-ups have a name for that trail: your **digital footprint**.

Some of that is genuinely helpful! It's *why* an app can remember your favorite show or pick
a song you'll love. That's the nice side. But it's also worth being thoughtful about,
because your information is a little like your **belongings** — and you should get a say in
who sees it.

Here's a way to feel the difference. You have a **diary** and you have a **class bulletin
board**. Some things you happily pin to the bulletin board for everyone — your favorite
color, a drawing you're proud of. Other things stay in the diary, just for you and people
you trust — your home address, your passwords, where you'll be on Saturday. Smart people
aren't scared of this; they're just *thoughtful* about which is which.

So here's your privacy power, three simple moves:

1. **Think before you share.** Ask, "Is this a bulletin-board thing or a diary thing?"
2. **Keep the diary stuff private** — your full name, address, phone, passwords, where you
   are. Those are *yours*.
3. **When you're not sure, ask a grown-up you trust.** That's not babyish — that's exactly
   what smart people do.

This isn't about being scared of the internet. It's about being the *boss* of your own
information — which, after Module 1, you already know is the whole vibe here. You're in
charge.

## Mission 8 — Who Holds the Biggest Tools?
*~10 minutes · Mission 8 of 12*

Here's a question that even a lot of grown-ups haven't stopped to think about: the most
powerful AI is really, really expensive and hard to build. So *who actually has it?*

Picture recess. Imagine one kid showed up owning **every single ball, every jump rope, and
the whole field.** Not because they're mean — maybe they're perfectly nice! But if everybody
else has to come ask that one kid every time they want to play… that's a little bit
uncomfortable, right? Even with a nice kid, it just doesn't feel *fair* for one person to
hold all of it.

Right now, the strongest AI tools in the world are a bit like that. Building a giant,
powerful AI takes enormous computers and a *lot* of money, so only a few big companies and a
few countries have the very biggest ones. That's not a movie villain thing — it's just how
expensive and new this is. But it raises a real fairness question, and you're old enough to
ask it: **if a tool can shape the whole world, is it okay for only a few groups to hold the
biggest ones?**

Why does it matter? Because tools shape what the world becomes. Whoever holds the most
powerful tools has a lot of say in *what gets built and who it's built for*. So lots of
people think it's important that AI doesn't stay locked up with just a few — that more
people, in more places, get to use it, learn it, and have a voice in it. (People like you,
learning it right now, are part of spreading it out.)

There's no bad guy in this Mission. There's just a fairness question worth caring about:
**the biggest tools should help everybody, not just whoever happens to own the field.**

## Mission 9 — Tools, Rules, and Freedom
*~10 minutes · Mission 9 of 12*

This Mission has the biggest idea in the whole module, and it builds right on the last one.
We're going to take it slow and start somewhere comfy.

**Good rules help everybody.** Think about a traffic light, or taking turns on the swings,
or raising your hand. Rules can be *kind* — they keep people safe and make sure everybody
gets a fair turn. A world with zero rules would actually be scary. So rules aren't the bad
guy here. Hold onto that.

Now the bigger idea. A super-powerful tool can be used **to help people**… or it can be used
**to boss people around.** Same tool, very different choice — just like the hammer.

Here's the friendly way to picture it: a **flashlight**. A flashlight can help you find your
way home in the dark — that's helping. Or someone could shine it right in your face to push
you around — that's bossing. Same flashlight. The difference is the *choice* of the person
holding it.

Powerful AI is the same. Used one way, it helps a city save energy, helps a doctor, helps
you learn. Used another way, a powerful tool could be used to watch people *all the time*, or
to control what they're allowed to do or say. In some places in the world, powerful tools do
get used to boss people around instead of to help them.

Now — that's not here to scare you. (If it feels like a big thought, that's okay; big
thoughts are allowed, and you can always talk about it with a grown-up you trust.) It's here
because it's the *most important reason* people care so much about **who controls AI and how
it's used.** This is exactly why the questions from the last few Missions matter: *should
we?* and *who gets to choose?* The answer the world is working toward is this: good rules,
and lots of different people having a say, keep powerful tools on the **helping** side.

And that's a hopeful ending, not a scary one. Tools don't decide how they're used —
**people do.** Rules, fairness, and many voices are how we keep the flashlight pointed at the
path home. And your generation gets to be some of those voices.
