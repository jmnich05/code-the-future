# Activity Bank — physical versions of every Module 1 concept

Unplugged activities, each mapped to the mission it reviews. Everything fits in one tote
bag and a library meeting room. Costs are per-cohort, mostly one-time.

**#1–10** are the original concept re-enactments. **#11–18** were added for the 90-minute
session — they fill the new **Mission Recap Showdown** (the game-show review Jon wanted)
and the **Make-It Lab** (the "leave holding something" segment), plus extra screens-down
options to rotate through the season.

## 1. Train the Human Robot 🤖  *(Missions 1–2 · the all-time opener)*
**Concept:** computers do exactly what you say — nothing more, nothing less.
**How:** Mentor (or a kid) is the Robot. Teams write step-by-step instructions for a task
— make a peanut-butter cracker, stack five cups into a pyramid, put on a jacket. The Robot
follows instructions *with malicious literalness* ("put peanut butter on cracker" → jar on
cracker). Teams debug and resubmit.
**Why it lands:** the funniest 20 minutes of the season, and they never again say "the
computer is being dumb" — they say "my instructions were fuzzy."
**Materials:** crackers/cups/jacket, paper, big marker. ~$10.
**Time:** 20–25 min. **Energy:** high.

## 2. The Sorting Machine 🐶🧁  *(Missions 3–4 · training data)*
**Concept:** training = examples in, rules out; test data finds your mistakes.
**How:** Teams get a stack of TRAINING cards (photos: dogs of every shape, then muffins,
mops, bagels) and write their "dog rules." Then the sealed TEST envelope arrives — with
the infamous chihuahua-vs-blueberry-muffin cards. Score correct calls; revise rules;
re-test. That's machine learning, on a table.
**Materials:** ~40 printed cards (we generate + print; laminate once). ~$15.
**Time:** 25 min. **Energy:** medium-high. **Print kit:** to be generated from the
platform's art pipeline.

## 3. The Human Neural Network 🧠  *(Mission 8 · the showstopper)*
**Concept:** signals flow through weighted connections; training = strengthening the right ones.
**How:** Kids stand in 3 rows (inputs → hidden → output) holding yarn between them. A
"signal" (hand squeeze) enters at the inputs. Single-strand yarn = weak connection (coin
flip whether the squeeze passes — mentor calls it); doubled yarn = strong, always passes.
Goal: get the signal to light the Output Kid (holds up a lightbulb sign). Then we "train":
the group chooses which connections to double, one at a time, until the network reliably
lights up. It is the platform's Light-the-Network game, made of children and string.
**Materials:** yarn ball, lightbulb sign. ~$8.
**Time:** 25–30 min. **Energy:** medium → loud at the end.

## 4. Pixel Detectives 🔍  *(Mission 5 · computer vision)*
**Concept:** to a computer, a picture is numbers — pixels.
**How:** Each kid gets a mystery card: a grid code ("row 1: 3 white, 2 black, 3 white…")
and a blank grid. Decode by coloring; the image emerges (a heart, a cat face, a rocket —
and one card decodes to the Code the Future spark). Then they ENCODE their own secret
image and trade with a partner to decode. Finished pixel art goes in the Time Capsule.
**Materials:** printed grids, pencils. ~$5.
**Time:** 20 min. **Energy:** calm-focused (great post-game cooldown).

## 5. The Next-Word Game 📖  *(Mission 6 · language models)*
**Concept:** AI writes by predicting the next word.
**How:** Circle story, one word per kid — round one in "boring mode" (say the MOST likely
next word; the story is hilariously dull), round two in "spicy mode" (say a *possible but
unlikely* word; chaos). Mentor names it: that's the creativity dial they used in the Word
Chain mission — same machine, different temperature.
**Materials:** none.
**Time:** 10–15 min. **Energy:** high. Perfect warm-up.

## 6. Attention Spotlight 🔦  *(Mission 7)*
**Concept:** attention = focusing on the words that matter.
**How:** A long, silly sentence on butcher paper on the wall ("The dog that chased the cat
that stole the sausage was very tired"). Lights dim. Mentor asks "WHO was tired?" — the
flashlight kid spotlights only the words needed. Rotate askers and spotlighters. Bonus
round: two flashlights for questions that need two clues.
**Materials:** butcher paper, marker, 2 flashlights. ~$12.
**Time:** 10–15 min. **Energy:** medium, slightly magical with lights off.

## 7. AI-or-Not Bins 🗑️  *(Missions 1–2 · physical arcade)*
**Concept:** AI learns and guesses; a machine follows fixed steps.
**How:** Photo cards hidden around the room (scavenger). Kids race to find them and drop
each into the AI bin or the JUST-A-MACHINE bin. Group review of the spicy ones (microwave
with a "smart" button? game enemies?). Same items as the platform arcade, so kids who
crushed the game get to be the experts.
**Materials:** ~20 printed cards, two labeled bins. ~$8.
**Time:** 10–15 min. **Energy:** maximum. Burns off arrival wiggles.

## 8. The Unfair Robot 🍎  *(Mission 10 · bias, judgment)*
**Concept:** bad examples → unfair guesses; humans are the judgment layer.
**How:** Mentor plays a "fruit-sorting robot" trained ONLY on red apples. Kids feed it
green apples, oranges, a red ball — it confidently gets them wrong ("RED + ROUND = APPLE").
Kids diagnose *why* (its training data was narrow) and fix it by supplying better
examples. Ends with the season's most important sentence, said by a kid, not us: "the
robot wasn't mean, it just didn't see enough examples."
**Materials:** plastic fruit or cards. ~$10.
**Time:** 15 min. **Energy:** medium, discussion-rich. [KENYA: this one carries the
fairness/feelings weight — review framing.]

## 9. History Timeline Relay 🏃  *(Mission 9)*
**Concept:** AI went from a room-sized computer to your pocket in one lifetime.
**How:** Big printed cards (ENIAC, Ada, Turing, ELIZA, Shakey, Deep Blue, smartphone
assistant — the same real photos from Mission 9) shuffled at one end of the room. Relay
teams race to fetch and place them in order on a clothesline timeline. Then each team
gets 30 seconds to "defend" one placement.
**Materials:** printed cards (from the platform's image set), clothesline, pins. ~$10.
**Time:** 15 min. **Energy:** high then focused.

## 10. The Time Capsule ⏳  *(Missions 9 + 11 · Kenya's signature)*
**Concept:** you are living inside the next chapter of the AI story.
**How:** Quiet ritual (see README). Prompt cards: *What is AI, in your words? What do you
use it for today? What do you think it will do when you're 16?* Write or draw; add one
artifact from today's session; into the capsule. Sealed at Demo Day, labeled "Open in 2031."
**Materials:** capsules (small jars/tubes, one per kid), prompt cards. ~$3/kid.
**Time:** 10 min. **Energy:** the calm landing the hour needs before pickup.

## 11. Mission Recap Showdown 🎮  *(any week · the game-show review)*
**Concept:** review last week's missions without it feeling like review.
**How:** 2–3 teams, mentor hosts, buzzer rings in. Round 1 Quick Hits (1 pt, straight
recall), Round 2 Spicy (2 pts, must give the *why*), a 30-second Lightning Round of rapid
AI-or-Not, and a Final Wager on one big "in your own words" question. Pull every question
from the week's missions; the scoreboard is the candy, the real win is each core idea
getting heard five more times.
**Why it lands:** it's the QA a parent asked for, disguised as the loudest fun of the
night. Slots into segment 2 every single week — only the questions change.
**Materials:** bell or buzzer, small whiteboard for score, question cards (one deck per
module). ~$10 one-time.
**Time:** 12–15 min. **Energy:** high. **Reuse:** every session.

## 12. Build-a-Bot 🤖  *(Missions 1–4 · the Make-It Lab headliner)*
**Concept:** a machine has fixed jobs; what would make it AI is the ability to learn.
**How:** Kids build a robot from craft supplies (cardboard, pipe cleaners, googly eyes),
then **give it exactly ONE job** on a tag ("sorts my socks"). The room votes: *follows
rules or learns?* and *could it be unfair if it only saw one kind of example?* (a soft
pre-tease of bias, activity #8). Bots go home or fold flat into the Time Capsule with a
photo.
**Why it lands:** the "what did you make today?!" segment. The concept lives in the *one
job* tag and the vote, not the glue.
**Materials:** cardboard, pipe cleaners, googly eyes, tape, markers, job tags. ~$20.
**Time:** 15–18 min. **Energy:** busy-happy.

## 13. "What Will YOU Build?" Mural 🎨  *(Missions 11–12 · the future is yours)*
**Concept:** kids are the generation that gets to *build* with AI, not just use it.
**How:** A long sheet of butcher paper on the wall. Each kid draws the AI helper they'd
invent for their own life — a homework buddy, a pet translator, a kid-sized robot. They
sign their panel. It becomes a wall display for the season and a great photo for parents
and socials.
**Why it lands:** collaborative, low-pressure, and it makes the abstract "future" concrete
and personal. Pairs naturally with #18.
**Materials:** butcher paper roll, markers. ~$8.
**Time:** 15 min (Make-It Lab) or 8 min (quick add-on). **Energy:** calm-creative.

## 14. Teach the Robot to Dance 🕺  *(Missions 1–2 · algorithms, with movement)*
**Concept:** an algorithm is a sequence of exact steps — including for a body.
**How:** Teams write a short dance as numbered instructions ("1. arms up. 2. spin once.
3. clap twice."). One kid is the "robot" and performs it *literally* to music — wrong
turns and all. Teams debug and re-run. Like Train the Human Robot, but loud, physical, and
a perfect warm-up.
**Why it lands:** gets bodies moving, and "the robot did exactly what we wrote" sticks
even harder when it's a dance fail set to music.
**Materials:** a speaker/phone for music, paper. ~$0.
**Time:** 10–15 min. **Energy:** high.

## 15. Act Like an Algorithm 🎭  *(Missions 2, 5, 6 · what AI does, as charades)*
**Concept:** AI systems each do a specific kind of guessing.
**How:** Charades with a twist — kids draw a card and *act out* an AI behavior: "be a
recommendation feed," "be a self-driving car deciding to stop," "be a chatbot guessing the
next word," "be a photo app finding faces." The rest guess which AI it is and whether it
*learns* or *follows rules*.
**Why it lands:** funny, physical, and it forces kids to articulate what each kind of AI
actually does.
**Materials:** prompt cards. ~$3.
**Time:** 10–15 min. **Energy:** high.

## 16. Move-Your-Feet Debate 🗳️  *(Missions 10–11 · judgment & fairness — bridges to Module 2)*
**Concept:** AI raises questions with no single right answer; humans decide together.
**How:** Mentor reads a kid-level question — *"Should a robot be allowed to grade your
homework?" "Should a self-driving car go if no one's around?"* One side of the room is YES,
the other NO; kids walk to their side and defend it. They're allowed to switch sides if a
friend changes their mind. No verdict required — the point is *thinking out loud and
listening*.
**Why it lands:** talking + bodies + real disagreement, on-ramp to Module 2's ethics. Keep
it light and concrete.
**Materials:** question cards, two signs (YES / NO). ~$3. [KENYA: review the question set —
keep stakes low and feelings safe; this one carries judgment/ethics weight.]
**Time:** 10–15 min. **Energy:** medium, engaged.

## 17. Library AI Scavenger Hunt 🔍  *(Missions 1–2 · AI in the real world)*
**Concept:** AI and "just machines" are both all around us right now.
**How:** A roaming hunt through the (pre-cleared) library: find things that *use AI* (the
catalog search, a voice assistant, a recommendation screen, self-checkout) and things that
*don't* (a paper sign, the clock, a drinking fountain). Kids snap or sticker a checklist;
regroup to argue the close calls.
**Why it lands:** gets them up and moving, and trains the everyday-AI-spotting habit in the
actual world instead of on cards.
**Materials:** checklist sheets, pencils; clear the route with library staff first. ~$3.
**Time:** 15–20 min. **Energy:** medium, mobile. [Confirm library permission + supervision
ratios before roaming.]

## 18. Design-an-AI-Helper 🏷️  *(Missions 11–12 · rehearsal for the Big Mission)*
**Concept:** invent an AI that solves a real problem — the operator mindset.
**How:** Each kid picks a problem from their own life (messy room, hard homework, lonely
pet) and designs an AI helper for it: draw it, name it, and give a 30-second pitch — *what
it does, what examples it would learn from*. It's the platform's Big Mission, rehearsed
off-screen and out loud, so kids walk into the capstone already knowing what they want to
build.
**Why it lands:** best run in the **session right before Demo Day**; the pitches become the
capstone projects.
**Materials:** design sheets, markers. ~$3.
**Time:** 15–18 min. **Energy:** focused-creative.
